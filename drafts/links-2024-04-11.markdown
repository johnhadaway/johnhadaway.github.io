- [Kiaochow](https://www.jstor.org/stable/3486727), a German leasehold territory (1898-1914) where a single tax, a LVT of 6% on the selling price of land, was implemented. 
- [An experiment](https://www.anthropic.com/news/collective-constitutional-ai-aligning-a-language-model-with-public-input) in soliciting public input using [Polis](https://pol.is/home) "to draft a constitution for an AI system."
- [Interesting observations](https://lloydalter.substack.com/p/why-living-on-top-of-the-shop-is) on micro-retail and "living on top of the shop." The article claims that Toronto's "property tax structure makes small retail unaffordable, which is why so much of it is being converted to residential." I wonder how property tax structures in cities I am more familiar with compare to Toronto's, and if they similarly disincentivise micro-retail? What other things, beyond tax structures, are at play?
- [This article](https://sfstandard.com/2023/12/13/san-francisco-chinese-cuisine-michelin-stars/) got me thinking about the Korzybski "the map is not the territory" aphorism. 
- [Polis in cities](https://nextcity.org/urbanist-news/the-digital-tools-unlocking-democracy-in-our-cities). excited to read about more experiments! 
- [A thought-provoking reflection](https://slatestarcodex.com/2014/11/21/the-categories-were-made-for-man-not-man-for-the-categories/) on categorisation.
- [This article](https://worksinprogress.co/issue/houston-we-have-a-solution/) on the history of Houston's land-use policy, which appears to have been largely succesful in provisioning affordable housing. The mechanism by which "Houston’s [opt-out] system places the cost of additional regulations on the people who want them imposed" was particularly interesting to think about. 
- [This article](https://worksinprogress.co/issue/upzoning-new-zealand/) on New Zealand's upzoning reforms. 
- [This article](https://joinreboot.org/p/searching-for-my-city?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email) on something I have been thinking about for a while: representations of cities on digital platforms, and the effects of such representations. It mentions [this article], commenting on how location-based digital services are "gentrifying neighborhoods" by making making services more "visible to upper-income patrons in other parts of the city"; it seems trivially true that such services have changed consumptions patterns, but I am not entirely convince that they and their information flow — and not something more structural, say, tax structures or land-use policy — should be the object of criticism. A slightly disingenous gripe, beyond the scope of the piece, perhaps.
